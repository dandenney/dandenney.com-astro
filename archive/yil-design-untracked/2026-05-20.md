## Overview

This draft is about a structural apprenticeship problem rather than a generic “AI changes coding” note. The 2026-05-20 inbox has one capture, but it is cohesive: Matt Pocock names the risk that AI removes the tactical-task bridge that historically let juniors enter software, and Dan's reflection turns it into a design challenge for teams.

The visual language should come from that bridge/ladder metaphor. Use a warm editorial surface with a broken middle span and intentionally designed new ramps: review, debug, spec, test, pair, strategy. The page should feel concerned but constructive — not anti-AI, and not overconfident that the answer is obvious.

## Reconciliation Summary

### Notable learnings
- Tactical tasks were not just busywork; they were the entry-level training ground where juniors slowly built strategic judgment.
- If AI absorbs tactical implementation, the industry may remove a key bridge before replacing it.
- Teams still need juniors and “new lifeblood,” so apprenticeship has to become a more intentional design problem.
- Potential AI-era junior work includes reviewing AI output, debugging, writing specs, testing assumptions, pairing on strategy, and gradual exposure to higher-level decisions.

### Possible YIL angles
- AI is not only changing who writes code; it changes how people learn to become software developers.
- The junior ladder may need to move from ticket assignment to deliberate apprenticeship design.
- “Review AI output” and “debug generated code” can be framed as judgment-building work if teams structure feedback and responsibility well.
- A page could ask how Dan would design a junior ladder inside an AI-native team or agent workflow.

### Links / references
- Matt Pocock on AI and the junior developer bridge: https://x.com/mattpocockuk/status/2057110441864696164

### Follow-up questions to preserve
- What does an “AI-era junior developer ladder” look like if tactical implementation is no longer the default entry point?
- Which replacement tasks actually build judgment instead of becoming another shallow checklist?
- How would Dan structure mentorship, review, and feedback loops so juniors learn strategy through AI-assisted work?
- Where in Dan's own agent workflows could a “junior bridge” metaphor apply: specs, code review, QA, debugging, or decision logs?

## Do's and Don'ts

### Do's
- Preserve the concern in Pocock's framing: the bridge into the industry may be getting pulled down.
- Keep Dan's open question unresolved; the page should invite a ladder design, not pretend the industry already has one.
- Make the design uniqueness emerge from bridges, ladders, missing spans, and replacement ramps.
- Treat AI as the context for redesigning apprenticeship, not as a villain or a simple productivity win.

### Don'ts
- Don't over-polish this into a generic future-of-work essay.
- Don't imply that juniors are obsolete; both Pocock and Dan's reflection explicitly argue the opposite.
- Don't publish or mark approved until Dan reviews the artifact.
- Don't add arbitrary visual variety unrelated to the apprenticeship bridge theme.
