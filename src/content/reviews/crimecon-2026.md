CrimeCon 2026 at Caesars Palace had the exact energy you want from a niche gathering that has fully grown into its own little universe. Big room, bright stage, true crime die-hards everywhere, and that specific feeling that half the people around you are here for the cases while the other half are here because this event has quietly become a reunion circuit of its own.

The food and drink situation was appropriately all over the place, which honestly fits Vegas better than anything polished ever could. You could graze, wander, talk for twenty minutes about one case, then get pulled into a completely different conversation before you even finished your drink. The whole thing moved with the loose, slightly overstimulated rhythm that conventions do at their best, enough structure to keep the day moving and enough chaos to let the fun parts happen naturally.

A big part of the fun was catching up with attendee friends built up over the years and getting to see Captain from True Crime Garage in the mix. That social layer mattered almost as much as the formal programming. CrimeCon works because it is not just people consuming stories, it is people building a shared language around cases, process, investigation, and the way all of that intersects with community.

What made the event stick was the balance between lurid subject matter and genuinely thoughtful discussion. The best sessions were not just recycling grim details, they were about how cases get worked, how information gets handled, and how people make sense of difficult material without flattening it into content sludge. Caesars gave it scale, Vegas gave it weird sparkle, and the whole weekend felt like a very specific kind of annual pilgrimage that somehow keeps getting both bigger and more personal.
