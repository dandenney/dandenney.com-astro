The pick was Wendell Carter under 1.5 blocks, which won the internal tiebreak because it looked like the cleanest single-stat under on a board with no real hammer. That was the theory, anyway. In practice it was still a blocks prop, which means the whole argument was balancing on whether one extra event showed up.

The signal was narrower than it looked. The final recommendation and Gemini backed Wendell. Claude and Perplexity both preferred Steven Kwan under 1.5 total bases. Smart Signal had Cade Cunningham under 45.5 PRA as the only other remotely respectable alternative. So the board was not giving clean consensus, it was giving two different versions of fragility: a spiky defensive stat under and a contact-hitter total-bases under.

The result was a loss for the featured pick. Carter finished with 2 blocks, which was enough to kill the under by exactly one. The martingale consequence was nothing, because no bet was placed in the 3-loss experiment. No reset, no doubling, just a recorded miss with no bankroll damage attached.

The learning is annoyingly plain. On this board, the lone `rebounds-under` went 0 of 1, while the three `hits-under` plays went 2 of 3. That is not a sweeping victory for baseball unders, but it is a useful reminder that a blocks under can fail cleanly even when the broader read is not absurd. The more important structural lesson is that Kwan also lost without extra-base power, just two singles. When a slate offers "safest" paths that can both be broken by small, ordinary events, the word safe is doing fiction's job.

The verdict is simple. The experiment's final side and Gemini both landed on Wendell and lost. Claude and Perplexity sided with Kwan and also lost. The board still finished 3-2, but the recommendation layer found the wrong 2.
